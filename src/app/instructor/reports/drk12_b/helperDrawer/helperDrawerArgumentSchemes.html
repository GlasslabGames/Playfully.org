STEP 1: Where Are We Going?

Familiarize yourself with how this skill fits into the practice of engaging in argument from evidence, and what it looks like when learners use it in the game and in real life. It's helpful to know what the learning target looks like in practice, before you review gameplay data, to get a sense of how far a student is from achieving the learning goal.

In Brief

Argument schemes are the underlying premises for common types of arguments, such as an “argument from authority.” When these premises are true, arguments are stronger because the logical connections among claim, evidence, and reasoning are clear and justified. Being able to identify argument schemes is valuable because all argument schemes have their own set of critical questions that help an arguer to evaluate the strength of an argument.

What does connecting evidence to argument schemes look like in the real world?

A student who is familiar with argument schemes is able to look at the evidence that someone uses to support a claim and determine what argument scheme that person is using.

For example:

Eric listens to his classmate Lauren make the following argument. “Global climate change will lead to lots of extinctions. Professor Deandra Sager says that Earth will lose one-sixth of all its species if climate change keeps going at this rate.” Eric knows that this as an “argument from authority” because Lauren used a piece of evidence based on what someone who seems to be an expert said. Knowing that, he can ask critical questions related to arguments from authority to determine how strong the evidence is, and therefore how strong the argument is. For example, he might ask, “Is Professor Sager actually an expert in this area?” Or, “Do other experts agree with her?”

More About Argument Schemes

The type of evidence used to support a claim determines what type of argument scheme someone is using. There are many types of argument schemes, but in the game players use four:

  1. Using evidence from an expert to support a claim leads to argument from authority
  2. Using evidence based on possible positive or negative outcomes to support a claim leads to an argument from consequences
  3. Using evidence from observation or experiment to support a claim leads to an argument from observation
  4. Using evidence that compares one thing to another to support a claim leads to argument from comparison

Every argument scheme has its own unwritten rules for making inferences from data to arrive at a conclusion (i.e., a claim). For example, in any “argument from authority,” one premise (usually implied) is that an expert is in a position to know about something. So, if someone uses a piece of evidence that's based on an expert's statement, then it's understood that this piece of evidence supports the claim because an expert is in a position to know.

Being able to identify argument schemes is useful because it helps when evaluating the strength of an argument. A strong argument is a persuasive argument. A student who can identify the argument scheme she is using (or that an opponent is using in a debate) can ask critical questions about how well the evidence supports the claim, which affects how persuasive it is. In the example above, the student asked whether the expert was the right kind of expert to be using as support for this claim. If the answer is yes, then the evidence is stronger, but if the answer is no, then the evidence is weaker. Asking critical questions is another skill that students practice in the game, and it is tied to argument schemes. When a student asks critical questions that are appropriate to an argument scheme, he or she can make an argument stronger, or can weaken that of an opponent.

What does connecting evidence to argument schemes look like in the game?

Each of the four argument schemes matches a type of argubot. Evidence that players use generally includes keywords or phrases that help to identify the argument scheme.

To match evidence to an argument scheme, students must consistently select evidence that matches the argubot type that they plan to use in a duel. There are two ways to do that:

1. Giving Lucas evidence that matches an argubot type to “unlock” or “level up” that bot

Matching evidence with argument schemes. To be able to play with each of the four argubots (a consebot in this example), players must first gather and hand to Lucas five pieces of evidence that fit the argubot’s argument scheme (argument from consequences in this example). Players complete the same process any time they wish to obtain a new bot or level-up an existing bot.

2. Pairing claims and evidence to build an argubot core

A good match. Players fuse cores by finding evidence that fits the argument scheme of the bot they are building. This observatron core has been correctly fused (the green lines mean a correct match) and is ready for battle, because the evidence fits with the argument from observation scheme.

An incorrect match. In this core, the evidence from consequence does not match the bot’s argument scheme (authority—seen at the top of the screen). The red lines mean that there’s a mismatch between argument scheme and the type of evidence.

STEP 3: How Do We Get From Here To Where We Need To Go?

After reviewing the student data, make a plan to help students move toward the learning target for this skill.

Why Might Students Struggle With This Skill?

  1. Although students might understand the basic concept of an argument scheme, they might have difficulty recognizing keywords or phrases that align with each scheme.
  2. While students might recognize the keywords or phrases associated with an argument scheme, they might not have a conceptual understanding of what an argument scheme is.
