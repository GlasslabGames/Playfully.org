Familiarize yourself with how this skill fits into the practice of engaging in argument from evidence, and what it looks like when learners use it in the game and in real life. In Brief

Backing is “evidence about evidence.” By ensuring that a piece of evidence is well-backed, an arguer has additional information that strengthens a piece of evidence that supports a claim. Having strong backing enables an arguer to defend her argument against critical questions from an opponent (another skill that students practice in the game).

What does using backing look like in the real world?

Students who use backing in their arguments understand that arguments are strengthened when they include additional information about the evidence. That information helps to answer critical questions about the evidence.

For example:

Martin makes the following argument from authority. “The dinosaurs were wiped out 66 million years ago when a giant asteroid smashed into the planet. Dr. Nabila Shaheen recently published an article that made that claim.” Martin knows that there are competing theories about why the dinosaurs went extinct, and he expects that his classmates will ask critical questions about his evidence. So, he includes additional backing about his evidence to support his argument by saying, “Dr. Shaheen is a highly-respected paleontologist, who has studied the question of how dinosaurs went extinct for over 20 years. Also, other researchers have published articles about the same claim with evidence to support the idea that it was an asteroid, and not volcanic activity over a long period of time.”

More About Using Backing

Backing is related to critical questions because it can be used to anticipate attempts to poke holes in arguments. By asking critical questions about one’s own evidence, an arguer can look for information to use as backing to make the argument stronger. Conversely, an arguer can ask critical questions and then determine that there isn’t enough backing to make it a stronger argument.

Using backing-—another skill that students practice in the game—is closely tied to the activity of asking critical questions. Backing is additional information that strengthens evidence and is a way to respond to critical questions. For example, in response to question #1 above, if a student provides information to show that an expert is world-renowned in a topic, then that student has strengthened her argument.

What does using backing look like in the game?

Backing is closely related to critical questions. Well-backed evidence makes an arguer’s evidence stronger and will hold up to an opponent’s critical questions.

In later missions, players can begin loading pieces of evidence into the “Backing Machine.” The machine resembles a slot machine, and dispenses additional information about the piece of evidence that a player loaded into it. Having that additional information about the evidence doesn’t necessarily mean that it’s well-backed, however. The player has to read the information carefully, as some of it will demonstrate that the piece of evidence does not support the claim. So, the player must read the backing information carefully and decide whether it helps to strengthen that piece of evidence for making an argument.

When a player uses evidence that has relevant backing, then his or her bot will be “shielded” from critical questions from opponents during duels.

Backing the evidence up. Students use the “Backing Machine” to add backing to a piece of evidence. While all evidence will have backing, not all backing will be good backing. Good backing must support the evidence, which enables students to defend against critical questions. Players need to read backing text carefully to make sure it’s good backing!

Defending against critical questions. Backing allows students to defend against critical question attacks. Using evidence with several pieces of valid backing allows students to defend against critical question attacks because the argument is made stronger by well-backed evidence.

STEP 3: How Do We Get From Here To Where We Need To Go?

After reviewing the student data, make a plan to help students move toward the learning target for this skill.

Why Might Students Struggle With This Skill?

  1. They might not anticipate that others will ask questions about the strength of their evidence (or “What makes your evidence true?”).
  2. If they do not understand differences among argument schemes, they might choose backing that responds to the wrong critical questions.
  3. Students might not read the information from the “evidence generator” carefully, and might use backing that does not support the evidence.
